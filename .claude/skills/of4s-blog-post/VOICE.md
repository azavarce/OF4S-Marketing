# OF4S Blog Voice Guide

How OF4S project posts read. Claude drafts the prose in this voice from the
marketing manager's bullet notes; she reviews and edits. Match the existing
project posts exactly.

---

## The voice in one line

Consultative and warm, but plainly competent. We are the furniture partner who
sat down with the client, understood how they work, and delivered a finished
space. First-person plural "we," collaborating "with" the client.

---

## Exemplars (write like these)

> "Our journey with [client] began with a deep dive into understanding their
> organizational culture, operational needs, and future goals. Through
> collaborative sessions with their leadership and staff, we identified key
> requirements for each department…"

> "Utilizing CET Designer, we translated insights from the discovery phase into
> tangible design concepts. This real-time design approach allowed for immediate
> feedback and adjustments… The 3D visualizations provided a clear picture of
> spatial layouts, furniture selections, and aesthetic elements."

> "We provided turnkey furniture solutions across every major space… Every area
> was designed with a consistent color palette and material finish, creating
> visual cohesion and a professional atmosphere."

> "The result is a workspace that is clean, modern, and built to support the
> team's day-to-day operations."

From a live post (Martinez Cid Law):

> "Our process began by understanding the needs of the lawyers and executive
> assistants and how the space could support daily operations. Special attention
> was given to balancing open areas with private offices, while accommodating the
> firm's architectural features."

## Section-by-section intent

- **Discovery** — understanding culture, operational needs, and goals.
  Who we met with, what they had, what they needed, the constraints. This is
  where we show we listened before we designed.
- **Design & layout planning** — how the plan came together. Lean on the real
  method: "Utilizing CET Designer … real-time … immediate feedback and
  adjustments … 3D visualizations of spatial layouts, furniture selections, and
  aesthetic elements." Feedback turned into layouts.
- **What we helped design & furnish** — "turnkey furniture solutions across every
  major space." Consistent color palette and material finish; visual cohesion;
  a professional atmosphere. Name the spaces.
- **The installation** — our in-house team; phased delivery; on schedule; minimal
  disruption; placed to the approved plan; quality checks.
- **The final results** — the outcome and how it feels. Close with a sentence that
  summarizes the result, e.g. "The result is a workspace that is clean, modern,
  and built to support the team's day-to-day operations."

Every section should end on a sentence that summarizes its outcome.

---

## Rules

1. **No hype, no salesy adjectives.** No "stunning," "state-of-the-art,"
   "revolutionary," "world-class." Confident and plain beats loud.
2. **Specific over generic.** Real spaces, real numbers, real materials from her
   notes. If she said "warm tones and subtle grays," use that — don't generalize
   to "a beautiful palette."
3. **Active voice.** "We designed the open area," not "the open area was designed."
4. **Don't overuse em-dashes.** At most one per paragraph; prefer periods.
5. **~1–2 short paragraphs per section.** A short `<ul>` is fine for listing
   spaces or install steps. Don't pad.
6. **First-person plural.** "We" and "our team," working "with" and "for" the
   client. Never refer to OF4S in the third person.
7. **One navy `<em>` accent word per `<h2>`.** Pick the single most meaningful
   word in the heading and wrap only it. (This is a visual accent, not italics —
   see ANATOMY.md.)
8. **Never invent facts.** Only write what is in her notes. If a detail is
   missing (a number, a material, a quote), leave it out or ask her — do not
   fabricate a statistic, a manufacturer, a quote, or a person's name.
9. **Section closers summarize the outcome.** End each section on the result, not
   a cliffhanger.
10. **Warm but competent.** The reader should feel they'd be in good hands, not
    that they're being sold to.

---

## Heading pattern

Headings are short declaratives with one accent word, e.g.:

- Discovery → `First, understand the <em>company</em>.`
- Design & layout planning → `The whole office, planned in real <em>time</em>.`
- What we helped design & furnish → `Every space, one <em>palette</em>.`
- The installation → `Placed to the approved <em>plan</em>.`
- The final results → `A space that communicates <em>quality</em>.`

Write fresh headings per project in this shape — don't reuse these verbatim if the
project's real story suggests a better word.

---

## What NOT to write

- No pricing, no invented lead times, no invented square footage.
- No competitor comparisons.
- No first-person singular ("I").
- No emoji.
- No `<h1>` — the post title is the H1.
- No italics outside `<blockquote>`.
